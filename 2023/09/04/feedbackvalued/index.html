How to know your feedback is valued

Sep 4, 2023

A lot is written on how to give feedback to other people, with most advice oriented around how to frame the feedback. In some cases, you might be interested in evaluating if the feedback has been influential. The specific type of feedback that inspired this post is code review feedback.

I give and receive code review feedback every day. By now, the number of comments on my PRs is usually low, but that wasn’t the case when I first joined. I was spinning up on the team context and Ruby at the same time. My team taught me code style best practices, let me know if there was a preferred helper function to use over the random one I copy and pasted from somewhere else, and generally helped me level up on knowledge of the codebase.

Looking back, you can use average number of comments on my PRs as a measurable (albeit imperfect) metric that’s changed. However, more importantly, there’s a shift in my self-confidence and attitude. If confidence is a spectrum where 0 is complete lack of faith in yourself and 100 is arrogance, ideally you’re somewhere in the middle. Both ends are not good, but the middle is good because you have opinions (ideally good ones) that can move the needle, conviction that they’re right (or at least rooted in the right idea), and the humility to change them when someone pokes a hole in your argument.

If you ask any of the people who originally left comments on my code if I’d actually taken their feedback to heart, I think they would say yes; they now focus on the logical changes in my PRs, rather than code style nits. When they evaluate whether their feedback was effective, they can come to a conclusion.

It seems like an obvious conclusion that you know your feedback is valued if it’s implemented. After all, the idiom is “actions speak louder than words.” However, the inverse is fuzzy. Is your feedback unvalued if there’s no action taken in response to it?

There’re probably two camps here:

  1. Yes.
  2. It’s not that simple.

Typically, I begin writing with a conclusion already in mind. My blog posts are all attempts to explain to myself how I reached a conclusion, building up from the start. In this case, I don’t know which camp I sit in.
